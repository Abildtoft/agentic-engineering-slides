# Jenny Wen on design in the AI era

Jenny Wen, Design Lead at Anthropic (formerly Design Director at Notion, 12–15 reports). Key takeaways shared by Lenny Rachitsky:

1. Traditional design process breaking down. The discover-diverge-converge loop doesn't work when engineers spin up seven coding agents and ship a working version before a designer finishes exploring options.

2. Design work splitting into two modes: (a) supporting execution — consulting with engineers, giving feedback, polishing in code; (b) setting short-range vision — 3–6 months, not multi-year roadmaps. Vision work is critical because when everyone can build anything fast, someone needs to point the team in a coherent direction.

3. Build trust through speed, not perfection. Ship early, label research previews, iterate publicly. Brand degrades from shipping rough AND going silent — not from shipping rough alone.

4. Most overlooked hire: the "cracked new grad." Blank slates, fast learners, no attachment to legacy processes. Their lack of baked-in rituals is an advantage.

5. Chat as interface isn't going away — infinite flexibility. Hybrid future where models generate UI elements on the fly while chat remains connective tissue.

6. Jenny went from design director back to IC. Questioned whether middle management had a safe future; wanted hands-on time during rapid change.

7. AI will likely get better at taste and judgment. Designers may be holding onto "taste" as a moat too tightly. But someone still has to be accountable for what ships.

8. Three hiring archetypes: strong generalists (80th percentile across skills), deep specialists (top 10% in one area), cracked new grads (fastest learners, no baggage).

9. Figma still essential — best tool for rapidly exploring 8–10 directions on a canvas. Coding tools are too linear and create investment bias toward one direction.

10. Low-leverage work is often highest-leverage for managers. Dogfooding, repro-ing bugs, fixing small issues creates deep product familiarity and earns team trust.
